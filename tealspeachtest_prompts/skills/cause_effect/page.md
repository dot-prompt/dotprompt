# Cause Effect (Causal Modeling)

## Definition
Identifying and utilizing the cause-effect pattern where stating a cause implies the effect.

## Core Effect on Listener
Cause-Effect (also known as Causal Modeling) is a Milton Model language pattern that functions by stating a cause in a way that implies a particular effect. The listener's brain automatically accepts the connection between cause and effect without questioning whether the relationship is actually valid.

- **Role in Milton Model**: Creates the impression that one thing naturally leads to another, making suggestions feel inevitable rather than imposed.
- **Opposite Meta Model Pattern**: Requires establishing the actual causal relationship ("How does X actually cause Y?").

## When to Introduce
Cause Effect is a foundational Milton Model pattern for beginners. It's used in therapy to make change feel natural, in sales to present product benefits, and in leadership for vision casting.

## How It Should Sound
The cause-effect link should feel like a natural observation about the listener's current experience, not an arbitrary claim. The effect should be desirable and positive.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Too Obvious**: "Because I said so, you must obey." (Sounds like a command, not a natural cause)
2. **Illogical Connections**: "Because you blinked, you became more confident." (No plausible link)
3. **Negative Framing**: "Because you failed, you'll fail again." (Links to negative outcomes)
4. **Overusing**: Applying it to every sentence, making it feel robotic

## Two Micro-Examples
1. "Because you're listening, you're already relaxing."
2. "As you read this, your understanding grows."

## Template Variations
### Simple Cause-Effect
"Because you [experience], you [effect]"

### Temporal Pattern
"As you [experience], you [effect]"

### Conditional Pattern
"When you [experience], [effect happens]"

### Escalation Pattern
"The more you [experience], the more [effect]"

## Scoring Guidance
- **High Score (80-100)**: User demonstrates excellent cause-effect pattern usage with smooth, natural connections. The cause is based on something the listener is already experiencing, and the effect describes a desirable state. The link feels plausible and unforced, using temporal patterns like "As you..." or "When you..." effectively.

- **Medium Score (40-79)**: User attempts cause-effect but the connection feels somewhat forced or the cause is not naturally tied to the listener's current experience. The effect may be less desirable or the pattern is used inconsistently.

- **Low Score (1-39)**: No cause-effect pattern used, or the connections are illogical, too obvious ("because I said so"), or linked to negative outcomes. May use the pattern as a blunt command rather than a subtle implication.

- **What to Listen For**: Words like "because," "as," "when," "leads to," "causes," "the more... the more." Listener should be already experiencing the cause. Effect should be positive and desirable.

- **Watch Out For**: Illogical connections, negative effects, over-obvious commands, overusing the pattern to the point of feeling robotic.
