# Scope Ambiguity

Scope Ambiguity involves creating unclear boundaries about what words modify, forcing the brain to consider multiple interpretations simultaneously.

## Definition
Identifying and utilizing unclear boundaries about what words modify, forcing the brain to consider multiple interpretations simultaneously.

## Core Effect
The brain holds incomplete meaning while parsing scope. While the conscious mind considers "wait, which ones?", the unconscious processes both possibilities.

## Key Components
- **Unclear Modifier Boundaries**: What words modify what is ambiguous
- **Dual Processing**: Brain holds multiple interpretations simultaneously
- **Natural Grammar**: Both interpretations grammatically valid
- **Positive Direction**: At least one interpretation serves therapeutic goal

## Formula
[Modifier] + [Noun 1] + [and/or] + [Noun 2]

## Common Modifiers
old, new, positive, negative, happy, calm, disturbing, beautiful, concerned

## Examples
- "The old men and women." (old = both or just men?)
- "Your positive beliefs and attitudes will grow."
- "The concerned parent's children."

## Common Mistakes
1. **Negative Interpretations**: Creating unintended negative meanings
2. **Too Subtle**: Only one interpretation actually works
3. **Breaking Grammar**: Making it sound unnatural
4. **Forcing Ambiguity**: Not making both meanings work
