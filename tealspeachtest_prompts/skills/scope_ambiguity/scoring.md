# Scope Ambiguity - Scoring Guide

## Overview
Scope Ambiguity is scored based on the effective use of unclear modifier boundaries that force the brain to consider multiple interpretations simultaneously. Both interpretations should be grammatically valid with at least one serving the therapeutic goal.

## Scoring Rubric

### High Score (80-100): Excellent Usage
- Natural unclear boundaries about what modifies what
- Both interpretations grammatically valid
- At least one serves therapeutic goal
- Sounds natural, not forced
- Creates dual processing

**Indicators**:
- Modifier could apply to multiple nouns
- Both meanings are possible
- Positive direction maintained

### Medium Score (40-79): Partial Usage
- Attempt made but one interpretation dominates
- May create unintended negative interpretations
- Grammar may feel strained
- Too subtle (only one meaning works)

**Indicators**:
- Some ambiguity present
- One meaning more prominent
- Flow slightly disrupted

### Low Score (1-39): No Usage
- No scope ambiguity pattern used
- Creates unwanted negative meanings
- Forces unnatural ambiguity
- Ambiguity doesn't serve therapeutic purpose

**Indicators**:
- Clear modifier-noun relationships
- No dual processing
- Negative meanings present

## What to Listen For
- Phrases like "The old men and women"
- "disturbing thoughts and feelings"
- "positive beliefs and attitudes"
- Unclear what modifies what

## Watch Out For
- **Negative interpretations** - Breaks therapeutic direction
- **Too subtle** - Loses dual processing benefit
- **Breaking flow** - Sounds unnatural
- **Forcing** - Not organic

## Example Assessments

### Example 1: "Your positive beliefs and attitudes will grow."
**Score**: 90
**Reasoning**: Clear scope ambiguity. "Positive" could apply to both "beliefs and attitudes" or just "beliefs." Both interpretations are positive and serve therapeutic goal. Natural grammar.

### Example 2: "The old men and women gathered."
**Score**: 70
**Reasoning**: Scope ambiguity present, but interpretation A (old men and old women) is more prominent. Still works but less elegant than example 1.

### Example 3: "Your thoughts and feelings are changing."
**Score**: 5
**Reasoning**: No scope ambiguity. Clear that "your" applies equally to both nouns. No dual processing created.
