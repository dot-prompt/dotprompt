# Comparative Deletions - Scoring Guide

## Overview
Comparative Deletions measure the effective use of comparative adjectives or adverbs without specifying what is being compared. The key is leaving the comparison point ambiguous so the listener fills in their own meaning.

## Scoring Rubric

### High Score (80-100): Excellent Usage
- Effectively uses comparison words without specifying comparison point
- Statement implies positive change or improvement
- Creates personal relevance through ambiguity
- Pattern feels natural and conversational

**Indicators**:
- Words like "better," "easier," "more," "deeper" present
- No "than X" specification
- Listener can project their own meaning
- Natural delivery

### Medium Score (40-79): Partial Usage
- Attempts comparative deletion but specifies too explicitly
- May use "better than before" which breaks the effect
- Pattern present but not fully effective
- Some loss of personal relevance

**Indicators**:
- Some comparative words present
- May include explicit comparison ("than before")
- Delivery somewhat mechanical
- Partial effect on listener

### Low Score (1-39): No Usage
- No comparative deletion pattern used
- Uses explicit comparisons that break effect
- Uses negative comparisons ("worse," "harder")
- Completely off-topic

**Indicators**:
- No comparative words present
- Explicit comparisons given
- No ambiguity
- No discernible effect

## What to Listen For
- Comparative words: better, easier, more, less, deeper, faster, sooner
- Deleted "than X" portion
- Positive implied change
- Personal relevance to listener

## Watch Out For
- Over-specificity ("better than Tuesday") - Reduces score
- Negative comparisons - Reduces score
- Clashing comparisons ("faster and slower") - Reduces score
- Explaining what you're comparing to - Breaks the effect

## Example Assessments

### Example Response 1: "It's better to relax now."
**Score**: 90
**Reasoning**: Clear comparative deletion. "Better" without specification. Listener fills in their own baseline. Natural and effective.

### Example Response 2: "You feel better than you did yesterday."
**Score**: 40
**Reasoning**: Comparative used but explicitly specified. This breaks the Milton Model effect by giving the listener a specific comparison point.

### Example Response 3: "Nice weather today."
**Score**: 5
**Reasoning**: No comparative deletion pattern used. Completely off-topic.

### Example Response 4: "Things are getting worse and it's harder to cope."
**Score**: 20
**Reasoning**: Uses comparative forms but negative direction. This is the opposite of the intended Milton Model effect.
