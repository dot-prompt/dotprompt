# Comparative Deletions

## Definition
A linguistic pattern involving the use of comparative adjectives or adverbs where the "standard of comparison" is omitted, forcing the listener to fill in the blanks with their own internal experience.

## Core Effect on Listener
Comparative Deletions function by using comparative words (better, more, less, easier) without specifying what the subject is being compared to. This forces the listener to fill in the blanks with their own internal experience, making the suggestion universally applicable and harder to resist.

- **Role in Milton Model**: Creates vague yet compelling suggestions by bypassing logical resistance
- **Opposite Meta Model Pattern**: Requires specifying the comparison (e.g., "Better than what exactly?")

## When to Introduce
Comparative Deletions is a beginner-friendly technique used in therapy, sales, coaching, and leadership to create suggestions that feel personal to each listener.

## How It Should Sound
The comparison should feel natural and ambiguous—no "than X" should be specified. Let the listener's unconscious fill in the blank.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Over-specificity**: "You feel better than yesterday." (Specifies comparison point)
2. **Clashing Comparisons**: "It's faster and slower at once." (Confuses brain)
3. **Negative Comparisons**: Using "worse," "harder" instead of positive direction
4. **Explaining**: Telling listener what you're comparing to

## Two Micro-Examples
1. "It's better to relax now." (Better than what? Listener decides)
2. "You can go deeper." (Deeper than what? Listener decides)

## Template Variations
### Simple
"It's better to..."

### With Pacing
"As you [action], you can become more..."

### Complex
"And it's easier to... and more natural to..."

## Scoring Guidance
- **High Score (80-100)**: User effectively uses comparison words ("better," "easier," "more") without specifying what is being compared. The statement implies positive change or improvement while leaving the comparison point ambiguous for the listener to fill in.

- **Medium Score (40-79)**: User attempts comparative deletions but may specify the comparison too explicitly ("better than before") or use vague comparisons that lose impact.

- **Low Score (1-39)**: No comparative deletion pattern used, or uses negative comparisons ("worse," "harder"), or specifies the comparison explicitly breaking the effect.

- **What to Listen For**: Comparative words without "than" clauses, positive implied change, personal relevance.

- **Watch Out For**: Over-specificity, clashing comparisons, negative outcomes.
