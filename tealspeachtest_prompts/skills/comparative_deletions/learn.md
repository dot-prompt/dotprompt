# Section 1: Skill Overview

## Core Content
Comparative Deletions function by using comparative words (better, more, less, easier) without specifying what the subject is being compared to. This forces the listener to fill in the blanks with their own internal experience, making the suggestion universally applicable and harder to resist.

## Key Points
- Creates vague yet compelling suggestions
- Bypasses logical resistance
- Forces listener to provide their own baseline
- Makes suggestions universally personal

## Example
"It's **better** to relax now." (Better than what? The listener decides.)

## Comprehension Question
What makes comparative deletions different from regular comparative statements?

## Follow-up Guidance
If they struggle, explain: The key difference is the deletion of the "than X" part. By not specifying what you're comparing to, the listener's brain automatically fills in their own reference point.

# Section 2: Psychological Mechanism

## Core Content
The human brain is a meaning-making machine. When it encounters a comparison like "You can feel better," it cannot process the word "better" without a baseline. Because the speaker didn't provide one, the brain automatically retrieves a baseline from the listener's own memory.

## Key Points
- Brain seeks meaning and context
- Without a comparison point, brain creates its own
- Creates personal relevance
- Triggers Transderivational Search (TDS)

## Example
"You can go **deeper**." - The listener thinks "deeper than now" or "deeper than before" based on their own experience.

## Comprehension Question
Why does comparative deletion bypass the critical mind?

## Follow-up Guidance
If they struggle, explain: There's nothing for the logical mind to disagree with since no specific claim is made. The listener fills in the blank themselves.

# Section 3: Linguistic Structure

## Core Content
The formula for comparative deletions is: [Subject] + [Comparative Adjective/Adverb] + [Deleted Object of Comparison]. Key trigger words include: Better, Faster, Deeper, Easier, More, Less, Sooner, Further, Greater, Superior.

## Key Points
- Formula: [Subject] + [Comparative] + [Deletion]
- Trigger words: -er words, more/less
- Templates: "And it's easier to...", "You might become more..."

## Example
"As you listen, you can understand **more**." - Subject: you, Comparative: more, Deleted: than what?

## Comprehension Question
What are the three essential components of a comparative deletion?

## Follow-up Guidance
If they struggle, remind them: The comparative word is essential, and the deletion of what you're comparing to is what makes it work.

# Section 4: Practical Applications

## Core Content
Comparative deletions can be used in therapy to track progress, in sales to present benefits, and in leadership for vision casting. The key is leaving the comparison point ambiguous so the listener applies their own meaning.

## Key Points
- Therapy: "You're feeling **better** now."
- Sales: "Our product is **more** effective."
- Leadership: "We're moving toward **greater** success."
- Hypnotic: "You can drift **further** into comfort."

## Example
A politician says "We need **better** jobs." - Each listener imagines their own version of "better."

## Comprehension Question
How would you use comparative deletion with someone who is skeptical of your message?

## Follow-up Guidance
If they struggle, suggest: Use vague positive comparisons that the listener can't argue with because they're defining the meaning themselves.
