# Lost Performatives - Teaching Content

## Section 1: Skill Overview

Lost Performatives are statements of value or belief where the source of the judgment is deleted, making opinions sound like objective truths. The listener accepts the value statement as fact rather than opinion.

**Key Points:**
- Makes subjective values feel like universal truths
- Deletes the source of judgment
- Listener accepts value as objective fact
- Reduces resistance to value statements

**Example:** "It's good to relax before sleep." - Whose standard? Deleted - sounds universal.

---

## Section 2: Psychological Mechanism

When a value statement has no attributed source, the listener's brain assumes it's a universal truth. The deletion of "who says so" makes the statement feel like stating a fact about reality rather than expressing an opinion.

**Key Points:**
- Universal Truth Appearance: No source = must be objectively true
- Absence of Resistance: No one to argue with
- Value Absorption: Listener accepts value as their own

**Example:** "It's natural to feel curious." - Without attribution, sounds like a universal truth about human nature.

---

## Section 3: Linguistic Structure

The formula is [Value Judgment without Attribution]. Trigger words include: It's good to, It's important to, It's natural to, It's valuable to, It's wise to, It's helpful to.

**Templates:**
- "It's good to..."
- "It's important to..."
- "It's natural to..."

**Example:** "It's important to breathe deeply." - Value: breathing deeply, Source: deleted.

---

## Section 4: Practical Applications

Lost performatives can be used in therapy to install healthy values, in education to frame learning as valuable, and in persuasion to make suggestions feel obvious.

**Applications:**
- Therapy: "It's healthy to express your feelings."
- Education: "It's important to practice daily."
- Hypnotic: "It's natural to drift deeper."

**Common Mistakes:**
- Being Preachy: Overusing to the point of sounding moralistic
- Conflicting Values: Stating values that conflict with listener's beliefs
- Revealing the Source: Adding qualifiers that show whose opinion it is
- Wrong Context: Using values the listener wouldn't accept
