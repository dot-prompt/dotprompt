# Phonological Ambiguity - Scoring Guide

## Overview
Phonological Ambiguity is scored based on the effective use of homophones (words with double meanings) that allow both interpretations to land at the unconscious level while the conscious mind processes the primary meaning.

## Scoring Rubric

### High Score (80-100): Excellent Usage
- Natural, effortless delivery of homophones
- Both meanings are relevant to the therapeutic goal
- Speech flow is not disrupted
- Unconscious processes both interpretations
- Sounds like normal conversation, not wordplay

**Indicators**:
- Homophones like "see/sea," "wander/wonder," "son/sun" used naturally
- Both meanings serve the client's direction
- Delivery feels conversational, not forced

### Medium Score (40-79): Partial Usage
- Attempt made but incomplete or awkward
- May force words that don't fit naturally
- Only one meaning makes sense in context
- Pattern feels gimmicky or disrupts flow

**Indicators**:
- Homophones present but context feels forced
- One meaning dominates; secondary is weak
- Delivery sounds like searching for clever words

### Low Score (1-39): No Usage
- No phonological ambiguity pattern used
- Forces unnatural words into conversation
- Only one meaning is relevant
- Overusing to the point of breaking flow

**Indicators**:
- No homophones present
- Sounds like wordplay rather than therapy
- Breaks conversational rapport

## What to Listen For
- Homophones: see/sea, pause/paws, wander/wonder, son/sun, right/write, bear/bare, flower/flour, night/knight
- Both meanings should be valid in context
- Natural speech flow maintained

## Watch Out For
- **Forced words** - Reduces authenticity
- **Single-meaning context** - Loses the dual processing benefit
- **Gimmicky feeling** - Breaks rapport
- **Breaking conversational flow** - Signals to conscious mind

## Example Assessments

### Example 1: "You can see clearly now."
**Score**: 90
**Reasoning**: Clear phonological ambiguity with "see/sea." Both meanings serve the therapeutic goal of clarity. Natural delivery, sounds like normal speech. Unconscious absorbs both visual clarity and ocean/openness.

### Example 2: "You can rest and relaxation will follow."
**Score**: 45
**Reasoning**: Attempt at ambiguity with "rest" (rest + pause/paws), but only one meaning is emphasized. Feels slightly forced. Not a true homophone in this context.

### Example 3: "Nice weather today."
**Score**: 5
**Reasoning**: No phonological ambiguity attempt. No homophones used. Completely off-topic or no therapeutic embedding.
