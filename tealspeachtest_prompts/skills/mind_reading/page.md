# Mind Reading

## Definition
Identifying and utilizing statements about the listener's internal state that create a powerful sense of being understood.

## Core Effect on Listener
Mind Reading is a Milton Model distortion pattern that involves making statements about the listener's internal state without having been told. The speaker claims to know what the listener is thinking or feeling.

- **Role in Milton Model**: Creates deep rapport by demonstrating apparent understanding of the listener's inner experience
- **Opposite Meta Model Pattern**: Requires asking rather than assuming ("How do you feel?" instead of "I know you feel...")

## When to Introduce
Mind Reading is a beginner-friendly technique used in therapy, sales, coaching, and leadership to build instant rapport and demonstrate empathy.

## How It Should Sound
Confident but not arrogant. Statements should be plausible—something the listener could reasonably be thinking or feeling. Use permission markers ("probably," "might") when uncertain.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Being Provably Wrong**: Statements that are clearly false damages credibility
2. **Being Too Specific**: Missing the mark by being too detailed
3. **Presuming Too Much**: Assuming you know when you don't
4. **Using on Resistant Listeners**: Those clearly thinking something else won't accept it

## Two Micro-Examples
1. "I know you're curious about how this works."
2. "You're probably wondering what's next."

## Template Variations
### Confident
"I know you're..."

### Tentative
"You're probably..."

### Observational
"I can see you're..."

## Scoring Guidance
- **High Score (80-100)**: User makes confident yet plausible statements about listener's internal state. Creates powerful sense of being understood. Uses phrases like "I know you're...," "You're probably...," "Part of you..." that feel genuine.

- **Medium Score (40-79)**: User attempts mind reading but may be too specific and miss the mark, or uses uncertain phrases when confident statements would be more effective.

- **Low Score (1-39)**: No mind reading pattern used, or statements are provably wrong damaging credibility, or too presumptuous creating resistance.

- **What to Listen For**: Phrases like "I know you're...," "You're probably...," "Part of you...," "I can see you're..."

- **Watch Out For**: Being provably wrong, being too specific and missing, presuming too much, using when listener is clearly thinking something else.
