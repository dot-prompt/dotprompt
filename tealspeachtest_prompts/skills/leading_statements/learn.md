# Leading Statements - Teaching Content

## Section 1: Skill Overview

Leading Statements are the transition from pacing to leading, guiding the listener toward desired internal states through suggestion disguised as natural progression. After establishing rapport through pacing, the speaker gently directs the listener's experience.

**Key Points:**
- Provides "leading" after "pacing" establishes alignment
- Makes direction feel like natural continuation
- Uses permissive language
- Creates "yes" momentum

**Example:** "I notice you're sitting comfortably... and as you settle in, you might begin to notice a gentle warmth."

---

## Section 2: Psychological Mechanism

After the listener has agreed with multiple pacing statements, they're in a state of receptivity. The leading statement capitalizes on this agreement pattern, making the direction feel like a natural continuation rather than an external imposition.

**Key Points:**
- Built-in Receptivity: Agreement patterns create "yes" momentum
- Natural Flow: Suggestion feels like listener's own idea
- Permission Without Resistance: Permissive language makes direction feel optional

**Example:** "You're breathing... and you can let that breath relax you more." - The "and" connects pacing to leading seamlessly.

---

## Section 3: Linguistic Structure

The formula is [Pacing Statement] + [Permissive Transition] + [Leading Statement]. Trigger words include: might, can, may, could, might find, begin to, allow.

**Templates:**
- "And as you ___, you might ___"
- "And while you ___, you can ___"
- "And you might find yourself ___"

**Example:** "You're sitting there (pace), and (transition) you can allow yourself to relax (lead)."

---

## Section 4: Practical Applications

Leading statements can be used in therapy to guide trance, in coaching to move clients forward, and in leadership to facilitate progress.

**Applications:**
- Therapy: "You mentioned your goal... and you might start seeing ways to achieve it."
- Hypnotic: "You're sitting comfortably... and you might allow your eyes to close."
- Coaching: "You've done the work... and you could now see the next step clearly."

**Common Mistakes:**
- Leading Without Pacing: Jumping straight to leading without establishing agreement first
- Directive Language: Using "you will" instead of permissive "you might"
- Abrupt Transitions: Making the shift from pacing to leading feel jarring
- Over-Directing: Leading too forcefully instead of suggesting gently
