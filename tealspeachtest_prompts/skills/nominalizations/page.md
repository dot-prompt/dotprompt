# Nominalizations

## Definition
Identifying and utilizing the transformation of dynamic processes into static nouns, making change seem manipulable.

## Core Effect on Listener
Nominalizations transform dynamic processes (verbs and adjectives) into static nouns. Instead of describing what someone is doing, you describe what "exists" as a noun.

- **Role in Milton Model**: Makes abstract processes feel concrete and changeable
- **Opposite Meta Model Pattern**: Requires converting nouns back to verbs ("What specifically is happening?")

## When to Introduce
Nominalizations are an intermediate technique used in therapy, coaching, and persuasion to make problems seem changeable.

## How It Should Sound
The noun form should feel like a "thing" that can be moved or transformed. Use transformation language like "can become," "is changing," "will develop."

## Common Mistakes
1. **Using Concrete Nouns**: Not actually nominalizing—using nouns that were already nouns
2. **Too Specific**: Explaining what the nominalization specifically means, losing vagueness
3. **Wrong Direction**: Making the nominalization sound fixed rather than changeable

## Two Micro-Examples
1. "Your frustration can become curiosity."
2. "Your decision to change is already forming."

## Template Variations
### Possessive Form
"Your [noun] can..."

### Definite Form
"The [noun] will..."

### Existence Form
"[Noun] is happening"

## Scoring Guidance
- **High Score (80-100)**: Effectively transforms dynamic processes into static nouns. Creates sense of solidity and allows listener to project personal meaning.

- **Medium Score (40-79)**: Attempts nominalizations but may use concrete nouns, or be too specific about meaning.

- **Low Score (1-39)**: No nominalization pattern, or uses only concrete nouns, or creates fixed/unchangeable state.

- **What to Listen For**: Verb-to-noun transformations like "decision," "frustration," "understanding"

- **Watch Out For**: Using concrete nouns, being too specific, creating fixed states.
