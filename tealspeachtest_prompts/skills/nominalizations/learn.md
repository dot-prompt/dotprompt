# Section 1: Skill Overview

## Core Content
Nominalizations are the transformation of dynamic processes (verbs and adjectives) into static nouns, making change seem manipulable. Instead of describing what someone is doing, you describe what "exists" as a noun.

## Key Points
- Transforms verbs/adjectives into nouns
- Makes abstract processes concrete
- Change seems possible because things can be moved
- Listener projects personal meaning

## Example
"Your frustration can become curiosity." - Frustration becomes a "thing" that can be transformed.

## Comprehension Question
What is the difference between "you're frustrated" and "your frustration"?

## Follow-up Guidance
If they struggle, explain: "You're frustrated" describes a current state. "Your frustration" makes it an object that can be moved, changed, or transformed.

---

# Section 2: Psychological Mechanism

## Core Content
When you say "you're frustrated" (adjective), it describes a current state. But when you say "your frustration" (noun), it becomes a thing that can be moved, changed, or transformed. The listener's unconscious fills in what the "thing" means to them personally.

## Key Points
- Objectification: Abstract processes feel concrete
- Transformation Possible: Things can be moved
- Personal Projection: Listener defines what the nominalization means

## Example
"Your confidence is already there, waiting to be accessed." - Confidence becomes a thing that exists and can be accessed.

## Comprehension Question
Why does making something a noun make it seem more changeable?

## Follow-up Guidance
If they struggle, explain: We understand that physical objects can be moved or changed. When you nominalize a process, it becomes an "object" in the mind that can be manipulated.

# Section 3: Linguistic Structure

## Core Content
The formula is [Verb/Adjective] → [Noun Form]. Common transformations include: frustrated → frustration, learn → learning, decide → decision, understand → understanding.

## Key Points
- Formula: Verb/Adjective → Noun
- Templates: "Your ___ can ___," "The ___ will ___"
- Creates sense of a "thing" that can be manipulated

## Example
Process: frustrated → Noun: frustration → "Your frustration can become curiosity."

## Comprehension Question
What are the essential components of a nominalization?

## Follow-up Guidance
If they struggle, remind them: You need a verb or adjective that can be turned into a noun form, then language that suggests the noun can change or be transformed.

---

# Section 4: Practical Applications

## Core Content
Nominalizations can be used in therapy to make problems changeable, in coaching to frame growth as possible, and in persuasion to make benefits concrete.

## Key Points
- Therapy: "Your resistance is actually protection."
- Coaching: "Your motivation is ready to emerge."
- Hypnotic: "Your confidence is already there, waiting to be accessed."

## Comprehension Question
How would you use nominalizations to help someone who sees their problem as fixed?

## Follow-up Guidance
If they struggle, suggest: Convert their description of the problem into a noun form, then suggest it can transform. This makes change sound possible.
