# Conversational Postulates

## Definition
Identifying and utilizing questions that function as commands or requests while preserving the appearance of choice.

## Core Effect on Listener
Conversational Postulates are questions that function as commands or requests while preserving the appearance of choice. The listener processes the question and feels compelled to respond in alignment with the implied request.

- **Role in Milton Model**: Uses the human tendency to answer questions to embed commands subtly
- **Opposite Meta Model Pattern**: Requires responding to the question literally ("I'll stand instead")

## When to Introduce
Conversational Postulates is a beginner-friendly technique used in therapy, sales, coaching, and leadership to guide behavior without triggering resistance.

## How It Should Sound
Soft, curious, permissive. The question should feel like an invitation, not an order.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Too Direct**: "Will you sit down now?" (Still sounds like a command)
2. **Challenging Tone**: "Can't you just relax?" (Creates resistance)
3. **Questions Without Implied Action**: "How are you feeling?" (Genuine question)

## Two Micro-Examples
1. "Would you like to make yourself comfortable?"
2. "Could you notice your breathing?"

## Template Variations
### Would You Like
"Would you like to..."

### Could You
"Could you..."

### Might You
"Might you be willing to..."

## Scoring Guidance
- **High Score (80-100)**: Question form implies commands while preserving sense of choice. Natural and conversational tone.

- **Medium Score (40-79)**: Attempts conversational postulates but feels forced or unclear.

- **Low Score (1-39)**: No pattern, or tone is too direct/challenging.

- **What to Listen For**: "Would you like to...?" "Could you notice...?" Soft, curious tone.

- **Watch Out For**: Challenging tone, questions without implied action, overusing.
