# Conversational Postulates - Scoring Guide

## Overview
Conversational Postulates measure the ability to use questions that function as commands while preserving the appearance of choice. The key is using soft, curious question forms that imply desired actions.

## Scoring Rubric

### High Score (80-100): Excellent Usage
- Question form effectively implies commands
- Preserves listener's sense of choice
- Natural and conversational tone
- Clear implied action

**Indicators**:
- Soft question words ("would you like," "could you")
- Permissive tone
- Clear implied action
- Listener feels they chose to act

### Medium Score (40-79): Partial Usage
- Attempts conversational postulates
- Question form feels forced
- Implied action unclear
- Tone may be too challenging

**Indicators**:
- Some question form present
- Implied action vague
- Some resistance from listener

### Low Score (1-39): No Usage
- No conversational postulate pattern
- Questions don't imply action
- Tone is direct/challenging
- Creates resistance

**Indicators**:
- Direct commands given
- Challenging tone
- No sense of choice

## What to Listen For
- "Would you like to...?"
- "Could you notice...?"
- Soft, curious tone
- Implied action

## Watch Out For
- Challenging tone - Reduces score
- Questions without implied action - Reduces score
- Overusing pattern - Sounds manipulative
- Too direct despite question form - Reduces score

## Example Assessments

### Example 1: "Would you like to make yourself comfortable?"
**Score**: 90
**Reasoning**: Clear conversational postulate. Question form with implied action. Natural and soft.

### Example 2: "Will you sit down now?"
**Score**: 40
**Reasoning**: Question form but sounds like command. Too direct.

### Example 3: "Nice weather today."
**Score**: 5
**Reasoning**: No conversational postulate. Off-topic.

### Example 4: "Can't you just relax?"
**Score**: 20
**Reasoning**: Challenging tone creates resistance. Not a true conversational postulate.
