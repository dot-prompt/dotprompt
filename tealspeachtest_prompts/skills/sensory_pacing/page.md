# Sensory Pacing

## Definition

Sensory pacing is a communication technique that involves making statements describing the listener's current sensory experience using language related to perception (visual, auditory, kinesthetic). Because the statements are immediately recognizable as true, they create a pattern of agreement and psychological alignment.

## Core Effect on Listener

By acknowledging aspects of the listener's sensory experience, the speaker builds rapport and cognitive synchronization. Once rapport is established, the speaker can smoothly transition into leading statements.

## When to Introduce

Sensory pacing is a foundational technique for beginners. It's used in conversational hypnosis, persuasion, therapy, coaching, negotiation, and guided meditation.

## How It Should Sound

Natural, observational—like you're simply describing what you notice. The statements should describe verifiably true experiences.

## Common Mistakes

1. **False Pacing**: Statements that don't match the listener's experience
2. **Overusing One Modality**: Using only visual language repeatedly
3. **Lack of Transition**: Pacing without leading
4. **Obvious Manipulation**: Sounding unnatural or scripted

## Two Micro-Examples

1. "As you sit there listening to my voice..." (kinesthetic + auditory)
2. "You can see the screen in front of you..." (visual)

## Template Variations

### Variation 1 - Simple Pacing
"As you [observable action]..."

### Variation 2 - Multi-Modal
"As you [action1], you can [observe1] and [observe2]..."

### Variation 3 - With Transition
"[Pacing]... [Pacing]... And as that happens, [Lead]..."

## Scoring Guidance

- **High Score (80-100)**: User masterfully uses sensory language matching the listener's current experience. Uses visual, auditory, or kinesthetic words that the listener can verify. Establishes clear agreement pattern through pacing, then smoothly transitions to leading suggestions.

- **Medium Score (40-79)**: User attempts sensory pacing but may use language the listener can't verify, or pacing doesn't match listener's actual experience, or transition to leading feels abrupt.

- **Low Score (1-39)**: No sensory pacing pattern used, or describes experiences listener can't verify, or jumps straight to suggestions without establishing pacing first.

- **What to Listen For**: Sensory words (see, hear, feel, notice, observe), statements about immediate experience the listener can verify, agreement patterns, smooth transition from pacing to leading.

- **Watch Out For**: False pacing, overusing one modality, no transition to leading, obvious manipulation.
