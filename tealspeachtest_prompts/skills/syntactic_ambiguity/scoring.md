# Syntactic Ambiguity - Scoring Guide

## Overview
Syntactic Ambiguity is scored based on the effective use of grammatical structures that allow multiple valid parses. The brain stays engaged trying to resolve structure while unconscious absorbs both interpretations.

## Scoring Rubric

### High Score (80-100): Excellent Usage
- Sentence structures with multiple valid parses
- Grammatically correct
- Creates engagement and dual processing
- Both interpretations meaningful
- Sounds natural

**Indicators**:
- Multiple grammatical parses possible
- Both interpretations make sense
- Creates the "gap" for suggestions

### Medium Score (40-79): Partial Usage
- Attempt made but limited ambiguity
- May be grammatically incorrect
- One interpretation dominates
- May be too complex or confusing

**Indicators**:
- Some parsing ambiguity
- Grammar issues
- One meaning more prominent

### Low Score (1-39): No Usage
- No syntactic ambiguity pattern
- Grammatically simple/clear sentences
- Creates frustration not engagement
- No dual processing

**Indicators**:
- Only one interpretation possible
- Clear grammatical structure
- No unconscious absorption

## What to Listen For
- "I saw the man with the telescope"
- "Flying planes can be dangerous"
- "They are visiting relatives"
- Multiple valid grammatical parses

## Watch Out For
- **Confusion without purpose** - Not therapeutic
- **Grammatical errors** - Breaks effectiveness
- **Too complex** - Loses both meanings
- **Breaking flow** - Unnatural delivery

## Example Assessments

### Example 1: "Flying planes can be dangerous."
**Score**: 90
**Reasoning**: Classic syntactic ambiguity. Two valid parses: (1) planes that fly are dangerous, (2) the activity of flying planes is dangerous. Both grammatically correct. Creates engagement.

### Example 2: "The cat sat on the mat."
**Score**: 5
**Reasoning**: No syntactic ambiguity. Clear grammatical structure with only one parse. No dual processing created.

### Example 3: "I go store the to."
**Score**: 5
**Reasoning**: Grammatically incorrect but not in a meaningful way. This is error, not ambiguity.
