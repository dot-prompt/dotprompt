# Section 1: The Psychology of Agreement Priming

## Core Content
Tag questions work by getting the listener to mentally answer the tag—which means mentally affirming the statement first. The brain processes "You're feeling good" and prepares to answer "...aren't you?" The preparation to answer "yes" creates psychological momentum in that direction.

This is different from a real question because you're not actually asking for an answer. You're using the tag to create internal agreement. The listener confirms to themselves that yes, they are feeling good.

## Key Points
- Brain prepares to answer the tag
- Mental "yes" creates momentum
- Tone matters: falling = expectation of yes
- Should sound conversational, not interrogative
- Works even without verbal response

## Example
"You're feeling relaxed, aren't you?"
- Statement: "You're feeling relaxed"
- Tag: "aren't you?"
- The brain answers "yes, I am"

## Comprehension Question
Why does the tag question prime agreement even without a verbal response?

## Follow-up Guidance
If they struggle, explain: The brain doesn't distinguish between preparing to answer and actually answering. The mere preparation to say "yes" creates psychological movement in that direction. Even if they never say it aloud, they've internally affirmed the statement.

# Section 2: Tone and Construction

## Core Content
The key to effective tag questions is tone. A falling inflection (voice goes down) signals "I'm expecting you to agree"—it becomes an embedded suggestion. A rising inflection (voice goes up) makes it a genuine question—and you lose the effect.

Construction: Match the tag to the statement's polarity (positive statement = negative tag; negative statement = positive tag).

## Key Points
- Falling inflection = embedded suggestion
- Rising inflection = real question
- Positive + negative tag: "You're good, aren't you?"
- Negative + positive tag: "You can't fail, can you?"

## Common Tags
- isn't it?
- aren't you?
- don't you?
- won't you?
- does it?
- didn't you?
- would you?

## Comprehension Question
What happens to the effect if you use a rising inflection on a tag question?

## Follow-up Guidance
If they struggle, explain: Rising inflection turns it into a genuine question, requiring an actual answer. The listener might say "no" or hesitate—and you've lost the agreement momentum. The magic of tag questions is the implicit affirmation, which requires falling inflection.

# Section 3: Practical Applications

## Core Content
Tag questions are used to gain agreement in therapy, sales, coaching, and leadership. They prime the listener before delivering the main suggestion.

## Key Points
- **Therapy**: "You're making progress, aren't you?"
- **Sales**: "This is what you need, isn't it?"
- **Leadership**: "We're on the right track, aren't we?"
- **Hypnotic**: "You can feel yourself relaxing, can't you?"

## Pattern Stacking
- Tag Question + Embedded Command: "You're comfortable, aren't you? And you can relax even more now."
- Tag Question + Presupposition: "You want to change, don't you? And that change is happening now."
- Tag Question + Conversational Postulate: "This feels good, doesn't it? Would you like to feel even better?"

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
1. Overusing (sounds mechanical)
2. Negative tags that invite disagreement
3. Rising inflection
4. Breaking flow (sounds like two utterances)
