# Tag Questions

## Definition
Tag Questions are statements followed by question tags that invite agreement, priming the listener to affirm the statement. The listener's brain processes the statement, then is primed to answer the tag—often in the affirmative.

## Core Effect on Listener
The brain processes the statement and prepares to answer the tag. By the time the tag arrives, the brain has already committed to the direction of the answer. A falling inflection suggests agreement is expected; a rising inflection makes it a genuine question.

## When to Introduce
Tag questions are a beginner-friendly technique used to prime the listener for agreement. They're used in therapy, sales, coaching, and leadership to gain buy-in before delivering suggestions.

## How It Should Sound
Conversational, with confident expectation of "yes." The tag should feel like part of the sentence, not a separate question.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Overusing**: Sounds interrogative rather than conversational
2. **Negative Tags**: "You don't want to fail, do you?" (invites disagreement)
3. **Mismatched Tone**: Rising inflection makes it a genuine question
4. **Breaking Flow**: Sounds like two separate utterances

## Two Micro-Examples
1. "You're feeling good, aren't you?"
2. "This makes sense, doesn't it?"

## Template Variations
### Variation 1
"[Statement], aren't you?"

### Variation 2
"[Statement], doesn't it?"

### Variation 3
"[Statement], can't you?"

## Scoring Guidance
- **High Score (80-100)**: User effectively uses statements followed by question tags that invite agreement. Tags like "isn't it," "aren't you," "doesn't it" prime the listener to affirm. Tone matches between statement and tag. Sounds conversational.

- **Medium Score (40-79)**: User attempts tag questions but may overuse, or use negative tags that invite disagreement, or mismatched tone between statement and tag.

- **Low Score (1-39)**: No tag question pattern used, or sounds like a real question requiring answer, or tags don't invite agreement.

- **What to Listen For**: Statements ending with tags like "aren't you," "isn't it," "doesn't it," "won't you."

- **Watch Out For**: Overusing, negative tags, mismatched tone, sounding like a real question.
